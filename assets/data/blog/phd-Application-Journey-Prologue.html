Why I Chose to Continue Along the Academic Path

My decision to continue along the academic path did not come from a sudden dream or a perfect plan. It grew gradually from my experiences during undergraduate and postgraduate study, especially from the moments when I tried to turn abstract ideas into working systems.

During my undergraduate years, I led a student science and innovation team. Together with my teammates, I worked on several projects, including an intelligent following system. Those projects gave me an early sense of what it means to build something from the ground up: discussing concepts, solving technical problems, testing different solutions, and improving the system through trial and error.

Whether the work involved software, hardware, or system integration, I genuinely enjoyed the process of exploration. I gradually realized that what attracted me was not only the final result, but also the process itself: starting from almost nothing and slowly building something meaningful.

That interest, however, was shaped not only by progress, but also by failure.

I once participated in a student robotics competition. My teammates and I spent nearly an entire month preparing for it, often staying up late to modify the system and solve unexpected problems. We placed a great deal of hope on the competition, but in the end, we failed because of mistakes in our work.

I remember feeling frustrated and disappointed at that time. Looking back, that experience taught me an important lesson: failure is always part of the journey. Research and engineering rarely progress smoothly, and success is never guaranteed. What matters is learning how to stay calm, keep a positive mindset, and continue moving forward when things do not go as expected.

Later, after being recommended for postgraduate study, I met Yaming Tian, a senior PhD student in my lab who mentored me during my early research training. He would graduate from his PhD one year later, and he had a strong influence on my early research experience. He taught me how to think about research problems, how to conduct experiments carefully, and how to face difficulties with patience.

I also assisted him with several experiments. Through that process, I became more familiar with the research process itself, not only as a set of methods, but also as a way of thinking: identifying problems, testing assumptions, learning from imperfect results, and gradually moving closer to a reliable answer.

One sentence he once told me left a particularly deep impression on me:

"There are always more solutions than problems."

I still remember this sentence clearly because it changed the way I viewed research and difficulty. Research is, in many ways, a process of meeting problems, understanding them, and resolving them step by step. Problems, failures, and uncertainty are unavoidable, but they should not become reasons to stop exploring. Instead, they can push people to think more deeply and improve more carefully.

Over time, I realized that I genuinely enjoy the life of research. I enjoy exploring unfamiliar directions, solving challenging problems, and refining ideas through experiments and reflection. More importantly, I find a sense of inner peace in pursuing something I truly care about.

Even when progress is slow or uncertain, I still want to continue learning, continue exploring, and go deeper along this path.

For me, pursuing a PhD is not simply about obtaining another degree; it is a continuation of my curiosity, my experiences, and my desire to grow through research.

Why I Started Thinking About Studying Abroad

My thoughts about studying abroad did not come from a sudden decision either. Instead, they gradually formed through the people around me and the experiences I observed over the past few years.

Two of my friends, Yujie Deng at KTH and LinHeng Kong at TUM, often shared parts of their study and daily life abroad through social media. Sometimes it was research, sometimes traveling, and sometimes just ordinary moments in another country. What impressed me was not the excitement itself, but the sense of independence and openness that slowly appeared in their lives.

At the same time, one of my mentors, Wei Xu, completed his doctoral study in Germany. During discussions and experiments in the laboratory, I could often sense a different style in the way he approached research problems: calm, rigorous, and highly independent. These were not things he deliberately emphasized, but qualities that gradually became visible through daily work and communication.

These experiences made me realize that studying abroad is meaningful not only because of academic resources or universities themselves. It can also place a person in a different academic and cultural environment, where the way one thinks, works, and makes judgments may slowly change.

I have always believed that the environment people live and work in, together with the experiences they go through, gradually shapes their perspective and abilities. More importantly, I also want to experience a different lifestyle myself - to step outside familiar surroundings, meet people from different backgrounds, and understand the world from a broader perspective.

Although I know this process may involve uncertainty and pressure, I still feel curious about the wider world and about the person I may become through these experiences. For me, studying abroad is not only an academic choice, but also a quiet journey of growth and exploration.
